The Everyday Sexism Project exists to catalogue instances of sexism experienced on a day to day basis. They might be serious or minor, outrageously offensive or so niggling and normalised that you don’t even feel able to protest. Say as much or as little as you like, use your real name or a pseudonym – it’s up to you. By sharing your story you’re showing the world that sexism does exist, it is faced by women everyday and it is a valid problem to discuss.

If you prefer to e-mail me at laura@everydaysexism.com I can upload your story for you instead. Follow us on Twitter (and submit entries by tweet) at @EverydaySexism.

Julefrokost

everydaysexism | 16th January 2019

I am a 22 years old woman and, as a foreigner, I started to work as a waitress in Denmark.
During these months I have established a great relationship with my bosses and we usually joke together and remain after work to chill or drink something together.
Last day we had our Julefrokost and I was so excited because I had never participated to something similar, so I was so happy to spend a funny and drunk night with my colleagues. We had a lot of fun and we ended up drunk and happy. During the last moments of the night I was dancing with my boss and I was having so much fun… except for the fact that at a certain point I felt touched by him, intensively. I imagine that unfortunately many people will know what I talk about when I mention that I was so confused and shocked that I realised it later and didn’t do nothing at that point.
After this experience I remain really shocked and incredibly angry. This, again, proves that in our world still exists the concept of the man/predator and the woman/prey. We still act as if it was normal because “well, you guys were drunk come on” and, still, this keeps happening always when the woman is in a vulnerable condition.

Anna

everydaysexism | 16th January 2019

2 years ago I graduated as a vet, making me Dr S-. A dear friend of mine graduated on the exact same day as a doctor, making him also Dr S-. We have the same friends and know each other’s family. We work in the same small town.

From the day of his graduation, no one has ever mistakenly called him Mr S-, and in fact they call him “Doc” as a nickname.

I constantly have to remind family, friends and work colleagues that I am not Miss S-. When I do, I am laughed at, or people comment that it is “snobbish” or attempting to make out that I am “better than I am” by insisting on the correct title.

This is so prevalent that even I start to wonder if I am making something out of nothing.

I wouldn’t even mind if they treated my friend the same, but they NEVER forget his title.

I hate how it gets inside my head and makes me feel like I’m in the wrong for insisting on the correct form of address.

Is it just me?

Schoolgirl

everydaysexism | 16th January 2019

I am thirteen years old.
I have had both men and boys catcall, grope and harass me already. When I was nine years old, on holiday in Turkey, I bought an ice cream with my family in a shop. The shopkeeper told me to come round the counter. “I don’t want to”, I said. My mum said ” don’t be rude”. So I went around the counter, and this forty-year old stranger kissed me. “He’s just trying to be nice”, my parents said.
I am thirteen years old, and already I know that an old man kissing a bewildered nine-year old is not nice. I know that the jokes boys in my class make about me are not funny. I know that catcalls about my boobs are not compliments.
I am thirteen years old, and I am a person. I am a girl, a bright beautiful girl who could run rings around half the boys in my class. I am not an object.
I am not a ‘that’.
I am thirteen years old, and my school does not teach about consent. In fact, the sex education we receive is less than adequate; a girl who received the same sex ed program as I do told me that the organizers passed a square of chocolate around the girls(only the girls) and said ‘would you want this chocolate now that everyone’s touched it?’
I am thirteen years old, and I am viewed as a joke for being a feminist. Ever since I sparked my first debate over the suffragettes in history, I have been the butt over countless jokes and jibes…’women don’t deserve rights’…(after I was held up on the stairs by boys in my class)’its funny because they’re holding the feminist back’…’feminazi’… Even the frankly hilarious ‘you and this ruler are the same: you’re both property’. Boys in my class make up stuff that they don’t even believe in order to get a reaction from me, because they think its funny to see a girl with political views.
The teachers do nothing to stop them.
I am thirteen years old, and already I have become sexualized and objectified by the fact that in the middle of June when its scorching hot and I have a long walk home, I wish to wear my shorts instead of heavy tracksuit bottoms. The boys in my school are of course permitted to do this. However, in the words of a senior sports coach, ‘girls who go out in the shirts are asking to be raped. Its child safety. You see girls going out, all cleavage and legs, asking to be attacked, and think ‘oh that is just awful’… I know it’s unfair that boys can wear theirs but boys are boys and girls are girls. You can’t change that’. Another teacher helpfully chipped in with ‘girls who go out in their shorts look like they’re only wearing knickers’.
Oh, the wisdom.
I am thirteen years old, but I am denied the right to play rugby. Half of my year has the opportunity to develop skills and teamwork skills, build up fitness, increase strength and stamina, make new friends,and have a possible career in rugby. I am denied these chances not because of my ability or fitness. Because of my gender.
I am thirteen years old, and I know that everything I have listed above should not be happening. That is why I fight constantly with members of my class and will continue to do so. That is why to sate every single one of my English essays that I can choose the topic of has been related to the issues within my school as regards sexism, in the hope that one day she’ll read it and think, you know what? That is not okay. That should not be happening to my students. I should do something about it. That is why I bring my copy of Everyday Sexism to class every day, and read it excerpts to anyone who will listen.
I am thirteen years old, and I will continue to speak out. I will continue to dress the way I like and wear makeup because I am not asking for rape, I am not ‘deliberately arousing male students’, I am displaying my confidence in my beautiful, flawed teenage body that no other person will ever have a right to other than me. I will continue to post things like this, because I won’t be silent and I’m not sorry.
I am thirteen years old. I’m not ugly or slutty or rude. I am not a slut, prude, tease, bitch, feminazi or ballbreaker.
I am my own person and I’m asking for change, I’m shouting back about the sexism that I face everday. I’m not what other people label me.
I am thirteen years old.
